Description

FAQ's


How did ACLJ start?

In 1990, Dr. M.G. "Pat" Robertson, a Yale law school graduate, religious leader, entrepreneur, and concerned citizen, decided to act to undo the damage done by almost a century of liberal thinking and activism. He founded the American Center for Law and Justice.


The ACLJ, in just a few years, has become this nation's pre-eminent public interest law firm and educational organization dedicated to defending and advancing religious liberty, the sanctity of human life, and the two-parent, marriage-bound family.

From the U.S. Supreme Court to local school boards, lawyers of the ACLJ have been defending the rights of believers, standing up for the unborn, the elderly and the infirm, and reasserting the primary rights of parents and the centrality of the family.

What is the mission of ACLJ?

The American Center has a national network of attorneys who are committed to the defense of Judeo-Christian values. They also cooperate with other organizations that are committed to a similar mission, and serve the public through educational efforts regarding First Amendment and religious freedom issues as well as pro-family and pro-life concerns.

What does ACLJ charge for its services?

Nothing!


As a nonprofit organization that does not charge for its legal services, the American Center for Law and Justice is dependent upon God and the resources He provides through the time, talent, and gifts of people who share our concerns over the erosion of our religious and civil liberties.

What is ECLJ?

A legal network of men and women working in the service of religious freedom and Christian values in Europe.

  • A Network of Attorneys and Jurists Throughout Europe
    • The ECLJ is, above all, an international network of men and women of faith, intent on defending religious freedom, respect for life, the family, and human dignity. It includes lawyers, professors, and jurists committed to the defense of Judeo-Christian values and the elimination of religious discrimination and persecution in Europe. They are active throughout many of the countries of Europe, and cooperate with other organizations that are committed to a similar mission.
  • Affiliated Associations: The SCLJ in Russia and the ACLJ in the United States
    • The battle for religious freedom and human dignity is being waged world wide. The ECLJ, the American Center for Law and Justice (ACLJ) in the United States, and the Slavic Centre for Law and Justice (SCLJ) in Russia, work together providing mutual support, exchange of information, and coordination of activities. 
  • The ECLJ defends:
    • Individuals and associations with respect to the protection and defense of religious freedom and fundamental human rights
    • Victims of religious discrimination or persecution from public authorities
      The rights of the unborn and the aged
    • The family

The principal office in Strasbourg, close to the European Court of Human Rights, coordinates the activities of the ECLJ. Through this office, cases can be brought to the European Court of Human Rights which has jurisdiction to protect the fundamental right to engage in religious worship, belief, and expression without fear of discrimination as guaranteed by the "European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms." The goal is to advocate before the court a jurisprudence that promotes the soul of Europe.
